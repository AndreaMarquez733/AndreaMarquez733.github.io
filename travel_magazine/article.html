The Hidden Valleys of the Swiss Alps

By Andrea Marquez June 12, 2025 8 min read

The train arrives in Brig from Zurich as the braking system releases air and the station clock signals the arrival time. By this point many visitors travel east toward Zermatt to view the mountain peak that has a jagged triangular shape, but I chose another direction. With a bus for local transit, I reached a valley opening in fifteen minutes that rental platforms do not list. At this location a board made of wood indicates the way to Gspon. It is a settlement without vehicles that sits on the slope at an elevation of 1,893 meters.

Switzerland is known because its natural being and rolling mountains throughout the country attracts photographers. It is also known because the social systems function with high efficiency. There is a different part of the country that is not part of the standard travel industry. In this area students use the trails of livestock to reach their classrooms. On those hills the metal bells on cattle are the primary noise after dark. And that is where I wanted to be.

Finding the Right Valley

Switzerland has twenty-six cantons and a thousand valleys it really depends on how you define them. The popular ones. Lauterbrunnen, Grindelwald, Engelberg. Are really beautiful but now their trails can get as crowded as airport terminals in August. I was looking for valleys so I did some research by talking to locals buying old hiking guides from second-hand shops and sometimes taking wrong turns on purpose.

The Lötschen Valley in the canton of Valais was my big discovery. You can only get to Lötschental by a train that goes up really steeply from the Rhône valley floor. This valley is home to four villages and has stayed isolated enough to have its own way of talking and a really strange tradition of wearing carved wooden masks during carnival. These masks are kept in farms and local museums. Have faces that are unlike anything else, in the Alps. They are weird, funny, really creepy, and somehow touching all at the same time. The Lötschen Valley and its masks are truly unique.

"The masks had faces unlike anything else in the Alpine world grotesque, humorous, deeply unsettling, and oddly moving all at once."
— Andrea Marquez, on the Lötschental folk tradition

Practical Notes

Getting to the known Swiss valleys is really not that hard. The Swiss Travel Pass is a good deal for longer trips and it covers trains, many buses and most boats. You should think of the bus networks as your first choice, not just a backup plan. They go to villages that the trains do not and the routes are often the most beautiful parts of the trip.

You can find a place to stay at any price if you look in the right places. The Swiss Youth Hostel network is modern and in locations. Some farms have a program called "Schlaf im Stroh”. This means you can sleep in the straw. Where you can pay in francs to sleep on a mattress in a working barn and breakfast is included. Guesthouses in the valleys that're not as popular tend to be much cheaper than the resort towns.

The best time to visit the valleys depends on what you want to see. If you go in June you will see a lot of wildflowers. The trails will be pretty empty. If you go in September you will get to see the alp descents, when farmers bring their cows down from the pastures. The cows are decorated with flowers and bells and the whole village comes out to watch, it is a very quiet and special thing to see in the autumn air. Both June and September are special times to visit the Swiss valleys. Both times are not very crowded. Both times are waiting for people who're willing
