Local Pickleball Business Hosts Alzheimer's Fundraising Event

Type Community News Story
Course JOUR 2310
Date March 12, 2026
Beat Lubbock Community

Project Overview

This community news story covers Lubbock Indoor Pickleball's monthly fundraising tournaments, held every fourth Saturday, to benefit Alzheimer's disease research. The event brings together local players who compete while raising funds and awareness for the Alzheimer's Association turning a growing recreational sport into a vehicle for community philanthropy.

This was my second formal news story assignment in JOUR 2310 and my first focused on a local community event rather than a developing news situation.

The Challenge

Community event coverage can easily become a press release dressed up as a news story. The challenge was to find the genuine human thread the reason this particular event matters beyond the fundraising numbers and let that carry the piece. I also needed to balance promotional quotes from the business owner with independent voices and verifiable context from health research.

My Role

I was the sole reporter and writer. I attended the event, conducted interviews on site with participants and the business owner, and supplemented those with data from the Alzheimer's Association, Mayo Clinic, and Pacific Neuroscience Institute research on pickleball's cognitive health benefits.

Reporting Process

I arrived at the venue during active tournament play and let the scene set the tone of the article the rhythmic sound of paddles, the rotation of matches, players catching their breath on the sidelines. That sensory detail became the opening of the piece.

From there I moved through a series of on site interviews:

  • Samantha Sanders, a contestant watching from the sidelines, spoke to the sense of community the event creates.
  • Regina Gallegos, an active participant, described her personal motivation for combining sport with advocacy.
  • Cindi Faulkner, the owner and manager of Lubbock Indoor Pickleball, explained the business's commitment to the Alzheimer's cause and the community more broadly.

I then grounded the piece in context: data from the Alzheimer's Association on its research mission, research from the Mayo Clinic linking daily exercise to reduced disease risk, and findings from Ryan Glatt of Pacific Neuroscience Institute on pickleball's cognitive benefits beyond just cardio.

Key Angle

The most interesting tension in the story is the connection between the sport itself and the cause it's supporting. Pickleball is emerging in research literature as a cognitively beneficial sport it requires spatial reasoning, reaction time, and social interaction. Fundraising for a neurological disease using a cognitively engaging sport isn't just a coincidence; it's part of what makes the event worth covering.

Final Story

The finished piece opens with a scene at the courts, establishes the monthly tournament and its philanthropic purpose, moves through player and owner voices, and closes with Faulkner's reflection on community turnout. The story stays human throughout while anchoring its claims in credible institutional and academic sources.

Reflection

Community journalism taught me something that breaking news doesn't: the importance of being fully present in a scene before you start asking questions. The opening image of this story paddles, courts, the hum of an event in motion only works because I spent time watching before I started writing in my notes. I also learned that the best community sources aren't necessarily the loudest voices in the room, but the ones who've been showing up quietly, week after week. Gallegos, wiping sweat from her face after a match, gave me the most grounded quote in the piece precisely because she wasn't there to be quoted.
